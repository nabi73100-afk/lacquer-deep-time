Chapter 5
Material Ontology and Technological Being: Reconsidering the Subjectivity of Materials
The preceding chapters have examined lacquer from the perspectives of polymer chemistry, composite engineering, and archaeological persistence. Together, these analyses reveal that lacquer possesses an unusual capacity to endure across extended temporal scales. However, the implications of this durability extend beyond materials science. The persistence of lacquer artifacts raises a broader philosophical question: can materials themselves participate in the formation of historical and cultural structures?
This chapter approaches the question through the framework of material ontology and technological being. By reconsidering the ontological status of materials, it becomes possible to understand how certain substances transcend their conventional roles as passive carriers of form and instead become active mediators of civilizational processes.
5.1 From Material Object to Material System
In traditional craft discourse, lacquer is typically described as a coating applied to pre-existing objects. Such descriptions implicitly position the material as subordinate to form. The object exists first, and the material merely enhances its durability or appearance.
Yet the emergence of dry-lacquer structures demonstrates that this hierarchy can be reversed. When textile-reinforced lacquer shells replace wooden cores, the material no longer functions as an accessory layer. Instead, it becomes the structural logic through which the object is generated. Form is not imposed upon lacquer; rather, lacquer itself becomes the medium through which form emerges.
This transformation suggests that materials should be understood not as isolated substances but as dynamic systems composed of chemical structures, fabrication techniques, and environmental interactions. In this sense, lacquer is better described as a material system—a network of processes that collectively produce stability, visual depth, and temporal persistence.
From this perspective, the ontological status of lacquer changes fundamentally. It is no longer simply a physical substance but a technological configuration capable of organizing matter, form, and time.
5.2 Technological Being and the Agency of Materials
Philosophical discussions of technology have increasingly emphasized the distributed nature of agency within technical systems. Rather than attributing causality solely to human intention, scholars have argued that technological processes emerge from complex interactions between humans, materials, and environments.
Within this framework, lacquer’s durability may be interpreted as a form of technological agency. The polymerization of urushiol, the reinforcement provided by textile fibers, and the stratified layering of coatings together create a material configuration capable of resisting entropy over long durations. These properties allow lacquer objects to shape the archaeological record by determining which artifacts survive and which disappear.
Such agency does not imply consciousness or intentionality on the part of materials. Instead, it reflects the capacity of material systems to influence the conditions under which cultural memory is preserved. By stabilizing forms and surfaces across centuries, lacquer participates in the transmission of aesthetic and technological knowledge.
The concept of technological being thus reframes the relationship between humans and materials. Rather than acting solely as instruments of human creativity, materials contribute their own structural possibilities and constraints. Human artisans respond to these possibilities through experimentation and craft practice, gradually developing techniques that amplify the material’s inherent properties.
In the case of lacquer, this process resulted in the discovery of its potential as both protective coating and structural composite. The resulting technologies exemplify how human–material collaboration can produce systems capable of enduring beyond the lifespan of individual cultures.
5.3 Material Subjectivity and the Reversal of Hierarchy
The persistence of lacquer films in archaeological contexts suggests that materials can acquire a form of subjectivity within technological systems. Here, subjectivity does not refer to consciousness but to the capacity of a material configuration to function as a stable center of structural organization.
When lacquer coatings outlast their wooden substrates, the hierarchy between support and surface is effectively reversed. What was originally conceived as an exterior layer becomes the primary surviving element, while the supposed structural core vanishes. In such cases, the “skin” of the object becomes its enduring body.
This reversal highlights the contingency of conventional material hierarchies. Engineering disciplines often assume that structural elements must be thicker, stronger, or more massive than surface layers. Yet dry-lacquer technologies demonstrate that thin stratified composites can achieve remarkable stability when properly configured.
The philosophical significance of this phenomenon lies in its challenge to substance-based ontologies. Rather than defining objects through a single dominant material, lacquer artifacts reveal that structural identity may emerge from the interaction of multiple layers and processes. Material subjectivity, therefore, arises not from intrinsic essence but from the dynamic configuration of material systems.
5.4 The Transition from Physical Materiality to Digital Materiality
The concept of material subjectivity becomes particularly relevant in the context of digital technologies. Advances in computational rendering and simulation have made it possible to replicate the visual and optical properties of materials with increasing accuracy. Techniques such as physically based rendering model the interaction of light with layered surfaces, producing images that mimic the depth and reflectivity of lacquer coatings.
In digital environments, materials exist not as physical substances but as parameterized systems. Their properties—color, reflectivity, roughness, and translucency—are encoded within algorithms that simulate the behavior of light and surface interactions. This transformation parallels the earlier shift from coating to structural medium in dry-lacquer technologies.
Just as lacquer evolved from decorative surface to load-bearing structure, digital representations of lacquer evolve from visual approximation to aesthetic system. Artists and designers working with computational tools increasingly treat material simulations as generative elements capable of shaping entire visual environments.
In this sense, the deep-time logic of lacquer extends into the digital domain. The layered optical depth characteristic of lacquer surfaces becomes a template for digital aesthetic structures, linking ancient craft knowledge with contemporary computational practices.
5.5 Toward a Material Ontology of Civilization
The philosophical implications of lacquer persistence ultimately point toward a broader rethinking of civilizational history. Instead of viewing civilizations primarily through political institutions or textual traditions, it becomes possible to examine the material infrastructures that sustain cultural continuity.
Materials such as lacquer play a crucial role in this process. Their durability stabilizes artifacts, their visual properties shape aesthetic traditions, and their technological requirements generate networks of craft knowledge. Over time, these factors combine to produce material environments that influence how societies construct memory and identity.
A material ontology of civilization therefore recognizes that historical continuity depends not only on human action but also on the capacities of materials themselves. Certain substances, through their unique properties, become carriers of temporal depth. They enable cultural forms to persist, adapt, and reappear across generations.
Lacquer exemplifies this phenomenon. Its transformation from protective coating to structural composite, and potentially to digital aesthetic system, illustrates how materials can traverse multiple ontological domains. By mediating between chemistry, technology, and culture, lacquer occupies a distinctive position within the broader history of material civilization.
