Introduction
Material Persistence and the Archaeological Problem of Lacquer Films
The long-term preservation of organic materials has remained a central problem in both materials science and archaeological conservation. Organic substrates such as wood, textile, and leather typically undergo progressive degradation through microbial activity, hydrolysis, and environmental fluctuations. Yet across East Asian archaeological contexts, lacquer coatings frequently demonstrate a striking anomaly: they persist structurally even when the original organic core has disappeared. This phenomenon, herein termed the “independent persistence of lacquer films,” challenges conventional models of organic material decay and demands reconsideration of the temporal behavior of polymeric natural materials.
Lacquer, derived primarily from the sap of Toxicodendron vernicifluum, is composed largely of urushiol, a catechol-based phenolic compound capable of oxidative polymerization. Upon curing, urushiol forms a highly cross-linked network with notable resistance to water, microbial attack, and chemical degradation. Studies in polymer chemistry have demonstrated that the resulting film exhibits both thermoset-like mechanical stability and remarkable environmental durability (Mills and White 146–152). Such properties partly explain why lacquer coatings survive in burial environments where untreated organic materials fail.
However, the archaeological record suggests that lacquer persistence cannot be understood solely in terms of molecular chemistry. In numerous excavated artifacts from China, Korea, and Japan, lacquer layers maintain coherent structural integrity despite the loss of the original substrate. Excavations at sites such as Mawangdui and the Nanyue King’s tomb have revealed lacquer objects in which the underlying wood had entirely decayed, leaving hollow yet intact lacquer shells (Barnes 203). This condition indicates that lacquer, under certain technological configurations, transcends its initial role as a protective coating and assumes a load-bearing or form-generating function.
The emergence of dry-lacquer (jia zhu or kanshitsu) techniques marks a critical historical transition in this process. By combining textile reinforcement with multiple lacquer layers, artisans effectively produced composite shell structures independent of solid cores. From a materials science perspective, such techniques represent an early form of fiber-reinforced polymer construction, predating modern synthetic composites by more than a millennium. The structural autonomy achieved through dry-lacquer methods suggests a shift in material hierarchy: lacquer transitions from a secondary protective layer to a primary structural medium.
This transformation has broader implications for the study of material temporality. Conventional conservation science often treats materials as passive carriers of cultural meaning, subject primarily to degradation processes. Yet the persistence of lacquer films implies an alternative model in which certain materials actively mediate historical duration. Their capacity to resist decay allows them to function as physical archives of technological knowledge and aesthetic systems across extended time scales.
Recent theoretical developments in material humanities and archaeomaterials research have begun to address such phenomena. Scholars have argued that materials should be understood not merely as inert substances but as dynamic agents participating in cultural and temporal processes (Ingold 14–17). Within this framework, lacquer’s exceptional durability positions it as a candidate for what may be termed a “deep-time material”: a substance whose physical properties enable it to traverse historical ruptures and maintain continuity between past and present.
Furthermore, advances in digital simulation and physically based rendering (PBR) technologies introduce a new dimension to the study of material persistence. The capacity to replicate lacquer’s optical and structural properties in virtual environments suggests that its temporal logic may extend beyond physical substrates. In digital contexts, lacquer’s layered depth and reflective characteristics can be reconstructed through algorithmic models, enabling a form of aesthetic continuity that parallels its archaeological persistence.
This paper therefore proposes a dual investigation. First, it examines the materials science mechanisms underlying the independent preservation of lacquer films, focusing on polymer structure, environmental resistance, and composite fabrication techniques. Second, it explores the theoretical implications of lacquer as a deep-time material, tracing its evolution from protective coating to structural shell and, ultimately, to a digitally translatable aesthetic system. By integrating conservation science, materials chemistry, and theoretical analysis, the study aims to situate lacquer within a broader discourse on the temporal agency of materials in human civilization.
