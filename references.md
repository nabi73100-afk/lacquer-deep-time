Abstract
The long-term preservation of lacquer artifacts across archaeological contexts presents a significant challenge to conventional models of organic material decay. This study investigates the independent persistence of lacquer films as a phenomenon that bridges polymer chemistry, composite engineering, archaeological conservation, and material theory. By examining the oxidative polymerization of urushiol, the stratified fabrication of lacquer coatings, and the structural autonomy achieved through dry-lacquer composite techniques, the research proposes the concept of “deep-time materials” as a framework for understanding material durability in civilizational contexts.
The analysis demonstrates that lacquer’s exceptional resistance to environmental degradation arises not only from its chemical composition but also from cumulative craft practices that transform it from a protective surface into a load-bearing structural system. Archaeological evidence of hollow lacquer shells surviving beyond their original substrates suggests that certain material configurations possess a form of temporal agency, actively mediating the preservation of cultural forms.
Building upon this insight, the study further explores the implications of lacquer’s deep-time logic for contemporary technological environments. Advances in computational rendering and artificial intelligence enable the simulation of lacquer’s optical and structural properties, extending its aesthetic principles into digital domains. This transition highlights the continuity between traditional material knowledge and emerging digital materiality.
By integrating materials science with philosophical inquiry, the paper advances a civilizational theory of material persistence, emphasizing the role of durable material systems in shaping historical continuity and technological innovation. Lacquer is thus reframed as a paradigmatic deep-time material whose evolution from organic sap to composite structure and digital aesthetic model illuminates the dynamic relationship between material resilience and cultural survival.
